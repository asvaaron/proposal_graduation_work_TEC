\section{Conclusions}

This project focused on the evaluation of the impact of modifying the MLP head in a Visual Transformer-based Facial Expression Recognition model specifically using a challenging or \gls{in-the-wild} based on the \cite{zheng_poster_2022}. This allows to have a different perspective regarding how the Visual Transformers can help achieve a better performance when using an enhanced version of the classified head for \gls{in-the-wild} Facial Expression Recognition. 

Thought the work developed in the thesis, a new version of the head is presented as an opportunity to explore on how the final classification can help improve model predictability or, also achieve better results on specific classes or challenging classes. The head was integrated into the code with parameters but using the base of hyperarameters  that helps run systematic experiments adding different sizes of the model.

\textbf{Conclusion 1 - Head modifications can improve FER performance in a nuanced way.}


The experimental results partially confirm the research hypothesis. Across all six configurations, overall top‑1 accuracy remained stable $(\approx 0.923–0.928)$, indicating that the choice of head architecture does not drastically affect dataset-level accuracy. However, macro‑F1 and per-class metrics reveal that the enhanced head delivers consistent gains for specific emotions, especially in the Small and Base POSTER variants. Thus, while global accuracy is largely preserved, the modified head improves the quality of predictions at a finer granularity, particularly for less represented or more ambiguous emotion classes.

\textbf{Conclusion 2 - The enhanced head benefits minority and difficult emotion classes.}

Detailed analysis of class-wise precision, recall, and F1‑Score shows that the enhanced head improves performance for challenging categories such as Fear, which are both underrepresented and harder to discriminate. These gains are achieved without harming, and in some cases slightly improving, well-defined majority classes like Happy. This suggests that the combination of non-linear transformation, normalization, and regularization in the head helps the model exploit subtle facial cues that a purely linear MLP head fails to capture, thereby increasing robustness to intra-class variability and class imbalance.


\textbf{Conclusion 3 - Smaller POSTER variants with enhanced heads offer an efficient accuracy–cost trade‑off.}


The comparison between Small, Base, and Large POSTER models indicates that larger backbones do not yield substantial accuracy gains over more compact configurations when the classification head is properly designed. Training-time analysis shows that Small and Base models are significantly more efficient, while achieving almost identical accuracy and macro‑F1 as the Large model. Consequently, from a practical perspective, deploying a Small or Base POSTER model with the enhanced head is preferable for many \gls{in-the-wild} \gls{FER} applications, as it provides competitive performance with lower computational cost and resource requirements.


\section{Future Work}

The project allows to have a lot opportunities to outperform the Poster research head investigation \cite{zheng_poster_2022}


\begin{itemize}
\item A usage of a Multi-dataset for validation is recommended to allow review and review head changes performance on the proposed  head. This allow for a more valid experiment, an alternative datasetAffecnet\footnote{\url{https://www.mohammadmahoor.com/pages/databases/affectnet/}} \footnote{\url{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/fatihkgg/affectnet-yolo-format}} is used on the baseline architecture paper \cite{zheng_poster_2022}. 

Although RAF-DB was selected as the primary dataset for the thesis work due to its balanced annotations and widespread adoption in transformer-based FER research and state of the art papers, other commonly used datasets include FER2013\footnote{\url{https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/msambare/fer2013}} \cite{khaireddin_facial_2021} and AffectNet, which are often employed for large-scale or cross-dataset generalization studies.

\item  The enhanced head architecture explored in this work represents only one point in a broader design space. Future work could investigate deeper or multi‑branch heads, gated or adaptive activation functions (e.g., GLU‑based layers), residual MLP blocks, or attention‑augmented classification heads. Systematic ablation studies comparing these variants would help isolate which components (normalization, dropout, depth, activation type) most strongly contribute to performance gains on difficult emotion classes.

\item Although this thesis work relied on standard evaluation metrics including accuracy, macro‑averaged F1‑Score, precision, recall, confusion matrices, and learning curves, additional statistical validation techniques were also explored during development—such as McNemar’s test for paired model comparisons, bootstrap-based confidence intervals for F1‑Score, Cohen’s kappa for agreement analysis, and chi‑square tests on confusion matrices. These analyses were not reported in the final experimental section in order to remain aligned with the reporting conventions of recent FER literature, which predominantly presents aggregate accuracy and F1‑Score without formal hypothesis tests. However, the use of such methods provides richer information about the significance and reliability of observed differences. A valuable line of future work is to formalize and integrate these statistical tests into the evaluation pipeline, and to report them systematically across datasets and architectures, thereby encouraging more statistically rigorous benchmarking practices in FER.
\end{itemize}.
